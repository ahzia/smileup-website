Your Lean Canvas Workbook
This workbook is here to support you as you develop your project idea during your first year in the fellowship. You’ll work on sections of the Lean Canvas each month, learning, testing, revising  and improving as you go.
Here’s how it works:
1. Each month, you’ll focus on parts of the Lean Canvas. After completing the content on the bVLC, you’ll receive a clear action to take, your “Lean Canvas Action”. Use the slides in this workbook to explore: write down ideas, notes, research, and anything else that helps you think things through and apply what you’ve learned. You can add more section slides if needed.
2. Once you’ve gathered enough information and ideas, take some time to reflect and write a clear summary of that section. This concise version is what you’ll add to the Lean Canvas at the end of the month and share with your Venture Coaching Group during your group call.
3. This workbook is also a space where your Venture Coach (and other fellows) can leave comments and feedback. It’s a simple, organized way to show your progress and stay connected with your support system throughout the fellowship.
4. In the following months, you’ll move on to a new copy of the canvas, where you’ll add that month’s section. You should  also review and update previous sections. If your thinking has evolved or you’ve gained new insights, update the earlier sections directly (keep the previous versions as they were). If nothing has changed, simply copy and paste the sections from before. This helps you track your journey and make sure everything still aligns.Use this workbook as a space to explore, reflect, and grow.
Take your time, trust the process, and keep building. :)

I have always struggled with procrastination. I’m passionate about many ideas and plans, but often found it difficult to stay committed until the end. I realized that breaking goals into smaller, achievable missions — and making them rewarding — increases the chances of actually completing them.

This gap bothers me, because I know that if the same energy and attention could be directed toward real-world impact, the results could be transformative.
I want to contribute meaningfully: to shift Gen Z from knowing about issues to doing something about them; to support communities and NGOs to reach new supporters; to leave behind a legacy where environmental education isn’t passive, but interactive, inclusive, and empowering.

Your why
List your personal motivation. Revisit as founder problem fit

Climate change is one of the most pressing challenges of our time, yet younger generations often struggle to translate awareness into meaningful action. Studies show that 59% of Gen Z feel anxious about climate change (Amnesty International, 2022), but less than 25% actively participate in environmental initiatives (Pew Research, 2023). 
Despite being the most digitally connected generation, many young people lack accessible, engaging pathways to turn concern into sustainable behaviors. Current educational approaches often emphasize information but fail to inspire consistent action. 
Traditional awareness campaigns, workshops, and lectures rarely compete with the engaging design of games and social media platforms, where Gen Z spends an average of 4+ hours per day (Statista, 2024). As a result, even motivated youth procrastinate or disengage, perceiving climate action as overwhelming, boring, or something “one person can’t change.”
 On the other hand, NGOs and community groups working on social impact projects struggle to mobilize volunteers and maintain long-term engagement. Many initiatives lack the tools to reach younger audiences where they are most active - on their phones. 
This creates a gap between organizations needing support and young people wanting to contribute but not finding rewarding entry points. At its core, the problem is not the lack of awareness but the gap between knowledge and behavior change. Without innovative, gamified approaches to make climate action social, rewarding, and accessible, Gen Z risks remaining passive spectators rather than empowered changemakers.

Focus on the purple section(s) this month

Proposed Solution: A gamified social impact platform where Gen Z can learn about climate action through short-form videos and complete real-world missions created by NGOs or community organizations. Users earn “Smiles” (digital tokens) by volunteering or completing sustainable tasks, and can use them to support projects, join challenges, or unlock rewards.
Unique Value Proposition: A fun, accessible, mobile-first way for young people to take meaningful environmental action — combining the engagement level of TikTok with the impact of volunteering.
Key Features:
Short video project pages (TikTok/Reels style)
Gamified missions (daily, weekly, and community missions)
Earnable tokens (Smiles)
NGO dashboards for publishing missions
User impact profile
Leaderboards & community interaction
Why It’s Better: Unlike traditional volunteer platforms or static awareness campaigns, SmileUp creates continuous engagement, simplifies access to climate action, and makes behavior change rewarding and social.

Project Journal
December 

Focus on the purple section(s) this month

A focused mission system (for 1–2 community types) is more effective than a universal mission system. We assume narrowing to specific community types (e.g., climate NGOs & university clubs) increases clarity, adoption, and usability.
Organizations prefer structured mission templates rather than fully customizable systems. Instead of giving admins total flexibility, we assume providing predefined mission formats reduces friction and improves onboarding.
Users engage more consistently when missions are time-bound (weekly/monthly) rather than open-ended.
Removing complex token systems (crypto-style or multiple currencies) increases trust and reduces confusion.
Basic reward systems (XP + Smiles) are sufficient to motivate early adopters without financial incentives.
Admins can realistically manage missions weekly without heavy operational burden.
